How to Write Competitor Alternative Pages That Rank and Convert

The single highest-converting page type in SaaS SEO — and almost nobody writes them correctly. This is the exact framework Delta uses to capture buyers who are already looking to switch, with a full page structure breakdown you can implement today.

Nitin Carpenter
Founder, Delta

Why Competitor Alternative Pages Are Different

Most SaaS companies start their SEO with top-of-funnel blog content — guides about industry trends, explainers about broad categories, educational posts for people who might one day become customers. This content can rank. But it rarely converts.

The person searching "what is project management software" is at the beginning of their journey. They might buy something in six months. They might not buy at all. You are investing in someone who has not decided they have a problem yet.

The person searching "[Competitor] alternatives" has already decided they have a problem. They are already using a tool and they are unhappy with it. They have allocated budget. They are comparing options right now. The only question is: are you on the list they find, or not?

A user searching "[Competitor] alternatives" is 6–12 months further down the buying journey than someone searching "best project management software." They are ready. The question is only whether you show up.

That is the entire insight behind competitor alternative pages. The conversion rate difference is dramatic — we consistently see these pages convert 3–5x better than top-of-funnel content when they are built correctly.

What Makes a Good Competitor Alternative Page

Most SaaS companies that have these pages do them badly. They write a generic page that says "looking for an alternative to [Competitor]? Try [Our Tool]!" with a feature comparison table and nothing else. These pages rank poorly and convert worse.

A page that actually ranks and converts does three things:

1. It genuinely addresses why someone is looking to leave

Before writing a single word, you need to know why people actually leave your target competitor. Check G2 reviews, Capterra reviews, Reddit threads, and Twitter/X complaints. Find the 3–5 recurring frustrations. Build the page around those specific pain points — not a generic list of your features.

If the top complaints about Competitor X are "terrible customer support," "the pricing jumped 3x," and "the mobile app is unusable," your page should address each of those directly. Not by saying "we have great support." By demonstrating it — show response time SLAs, show pricing that stays stable, show your mobile app.

2. It is honest and specific, not promotional

The biggest mistake: writing your competitor alternative page like a sales brochure. The reader arrived skeptical. They are evaluating you, not buying from you. Pages that feel like advertising create distrust and bounce.

Be honest about what Competitor X does well. Be specific about what you do differently. Give the reader enough information to make their own decision. The pages that convert best feel like they were written by someone who genuinely knows both products — not by a marketing team that just wants the sale.

3. It is built for the specific search intent

Someone searching "[Competitor] alternatives" is looking for a list of options or a direct recommendation — not a company history or a feature dump. The page structure needs to match that intent immediately. The first thing they see needs to answer "is this the right page for me?" within 3 seconds.

Key Point

Google ranks pages that best satisfy search intent. If 80% of pages ranking for "[Competitor] alternatives" are comparison lists with multiple options, your single-product promotion page will not rank no matter how good the SEO is. Match the intent first.

The Exact Page Structure That Works

Here is the structure Delta uses when building competitor alternative pages for clients. Every section has a specific purpose.

Above the fold (0–300px)

The reader needs to instantly understand three things: this page is about alternatives to [Competitor], your product is one of them, and there is a reason to keep reading.

  • H1: "Best [Competitor] Alternatives in [Year]" or "Looking for a [Competitor] Alternative? Here Are the Best Options."
  • Opening paragraph: 2–3 sentences acknowledging why someone might be looking to switch. Validate the frustration.
  • CTA above fold: "Try [Your Product] Free" — soft, non-pushy, just visible.

Why people leave [Competitor] — the honest breakdown

This is the section that earns trust and sets up your positioning. Summarise the 3–4 most common reasons people leave the competitor, sourced from real reviews. Be accurate — if the competitor has genuinely improved something that used to be a complaint, acknowledge it.

This section signals to the reader: this page was written by someone who actually knows this product, not by a marketing team. That trust is what keeps them reading.

The alternatives — including your product

List 4–7 alternatives including your own product. Your product does not have to be listed first. Each alternative gets: a brief description, who it's best for, key differentiators, and a link to learn more or try it.

This is counterintuitive — why would you recommend competitors? Because the page that lists multiple options is the page that ranks. The page that is honest about which option fits which user is the page that converts, because readers trust it. You can position your product as the best fit for your specific ICP without pretending the others don't exist.

Common Mistake

Listing yourself first and giving your product 3x more copy than every other option. Readers see this immediately and it destroys credibility. Be proportionate. Let your positioning speak through accuracy, not through disproportionate coverage.

Your product — the detailed section

After the alternatives overview, give your product its own deeper section. This is where you go into detail about what makes your product specifically different — not just generically "better."

  • The specific use case or user type your product serves best
  • 2–3 concrete differentiators (with evidence — screenshots, data, testimonials)
  • Pricing — be transparent, even if it's higher
  • G2 or Capterra aggregate rating
  • A direct CTA: "Start your free trial" with the trial length specified

FAQ section

Add 4–6 questions that target related long-tail searches. Examples for a [Competitor] alternatives page:

  • "Is [Competitor] worth it in [Year]?"
  • "What is the cheapest alternative to [Competitor]?"
  • "Can I migrate from [Competitor] to [Your Product]?"
  • "Does [Your Product] integrate with [common integration]?"

Each FAQ answer is 2–4 sentences. These capture additional long-tail keyword variations and improve the page's topical coverage — which helps it rank for related searches beyond just the main keyword.

Finding the Right Competitor Keywords to Target

Not every competitor is worth building an alternative page for. Here is how to prioritise:

Step 1: List your 5–10 closest competitors

Start with the tools your prospects mention most often during sales calls, the tools that appear in your "how did you find us" survey responses, and the tools that come up in your category on G2 or Capterra.

Step 2: Check search volume for "[Competitor] alternatives"

Use Ahrefs or Google Keyword Planner. Look for keywords with at least 100 monthly searches. Lower than that and the traffic will be negligible even if you rank #1. Higher than 1,000 and the competition is likely too strong for a new page without domain authority.

The sweet spot for early-stage SaaS is 100–800 monthly searches with a keyword difficulty under 30. These are achievable rankings that will move trial signups within 3–4 months.

Step 3: Check current ranking difficulty

Search "[Competitor] alternatives" in an incognito window. Look at the top 5 results. If they are all DR 70+ domains (TechRadar, G2, Capterra, Zapier), breaking into the top 5 will require significant domain authority. If you see DR 30–50 company blogs in the results, this is achievable within 3–6 months of a new page going live.

Step 4: Prioritise by conversion potential, not just volume

A competitor with 200 monthly searches but a very similar product to yours is worth more than a competitor with 2,000 monthly searches but a different ICP. The closer the competitor's audience is to your ideal customer, the higher the conversion rate from that traffic.

Quick Win

Start with your closest direct competitor — the one your sales team hears about most often. Write that page first, get it ranking, and use the early traction data to decide which competitor to target next.

On-Page SEO for Alternative Pages

Once the content is written, the on-page SEO signals need to be precise. Here is what matters most:

Title tag

Format: "Best [Competitor] Alternatives in [Year] (Ranked & Reviewed)" or "[X] [Competitor] Alternatives for [Use Case] — [Year]". Include the year — Google tends to favour freshness for "alternatives" queries, and users click on recent results. Update the year in your title tag every January.

URL slug

Keep it clean and keyword-exact: /blog/competitor-alternatives or /[competitor]-alternatives. Avoid dates in the URL — you want this page to live at the same URL permanently and accumulate link equity over time.

Internal links

Link to this page from: your homepage (if the competitor is a primary one), your pricing page, any other alternative pages you have written, and any blog posts that mention the competitor. Internal links distribute PageRank and help Google understand this is an important page worth ranking.

Schema markup

Add FAQPage schema for the FAQ section. This can get your page a featured snippet in search results — a significant traffic boost for no extra content work.

What to Avoid

A few things that kill otherwise good alternative pages:

  • Making false claims about the competitor. If you say "Competitor X has no mobile app" and they do, anyone who reads that and checks will never trust you again. Accuracy is non-negotiable.
  • Thin FAQ sections. A FAQ with 2-sentence questions and 1-sentence answers adds no value. Each answer should be 3–5 sentences of genuine information.
  • No trial CTA above the fold. The reader who wants to act immediately should be able to without scrolling. A single, soft CTA above the fold captures this intent.
  • Forgetting to update the page. Alternative pages need refreshing when the competitor updates their pricing, adds features, or changes their target market. A stale page with outdated information will lose rankings and credibility.
  • Targeting a competitor your audience has never heard of. The traffic from obscure competitor alternatives is usually lower quality — users may be searching for research rather than purchasing. Focus on the competitors your actual prospects mention.

Summary: What to Do This Week

Competitor alternative pages are the highest-ROI content investment a SaaS company can make in SEO. The intent is purchase-level, the competition is beatable, and a single well-ranked page can drive 10–40 trial signups per month from a few hundred targeted monthly visitors.

Here is the complete action list:

  1. List your 5 closest competitors
  2. Check "[Competitor] alternatives" search volume for each — target 100–800 monthly searches
  3. Check the top 5 ranking pages — look for DR under 50 in the results
  4. Pick the competitor with the best combination of search volume + achievable difficulty + similar ICP
  5. Research real reasons people leave them using G2, Capterra, Reddit
  6. Write the page using the structure above — honest, specific, genuinely helpful
  7. Optimise title tag, URL, internal links, and FAQ schema
  8. Publish, submit to Google Search Console, build 2–3 internal links from related pages
  9. Check rankings in 6–8 weeks. Refresh and expand if not in top 20
